Awareness on Phishing

If you're working for a company and want to assess how many employees might fall for a phishing email, the best approach is to run an internal phishing campaign. This test simulates real-world phishing attempts and provides valuable insights into user behavior and the effectiveness of your security training.

The phishing campaign can be created in-house or through third-party vendors that specialize in these simulations. These tools typically track:

  • Email opens

  • Link clicks

  • Form submissions or other interactions

When a user clicks on a simulated phishing link, they are immediately notified that the email was part of a test and that their action was incorrect. They’re often directed to additional training—either online modules or in-person sessions—to help reinforce proper security practices.

The primary goal is to educate users to recognize and report phishing emails. Key indicators include:

  • Spelling and grammatical errors

  • Suspicious or mismatched domain names in links

  • Unusual email formatting or structure

  • Unexpected attachments

  • Requests for personal information or login credentials

This exercise also serves as a test for your email security systems. If phishing simulations come from external sources, it’s a good opportunity to evaluate whether email filters and anti-spam tools are working correctly. Ideally, these filters should prevent phishing emails from reaching the user inbox.

To strengthen awareness, users should be trained to:

  • Avoid clicking links in emails unless they are certain of the source

  • Never open attachments from unknown or suspicious senders

  • Report phishing attempts through a well-documented, easy-to-follow process (e.g., a dedicated "Report Phishing" button or email address)

By regularly conducting phishing simulations and reinforcing training, an organization can dramatically reduce the risk of a successful phishing attack and improve its overall security posture.

Anomalous Behavior Recognition

Not only are we monitoring for phishing attempts, but we’re also on the lookout for anything unusual happening on a user’s workstation. This is known as anomalous behavior recognition, and it plays a crucial role in detecting both malicious actions and human errors that could compromise security.

We begin by identifying risky behavior—actions that indicate a potential threat or policy violation. This can include:

  • A user or service modifying critical system files, such as the hosts file.

  • Replacing or tampering with the core operating system files.

  • Unexpected file uploads, which might indicate data exfiltration or unauthorized data movement.

We also look for unexpected behavior, which typically signals something out of the ordinary:

  • Logins from foreign or unusual locations, especially if they deviate from a user’s normal geographic pattern.

  • Sudden spikes in data transfers from a device, which could suggest data leakage or malware activity.

In addition, we must recognize unintentional or accidental behavior, often caused by human error:

  • Typing an incorrect domain name, which could lead to unintended access attempts or phishing sites.

  • Misplacing a USB drive, which could result in data loss or exposure.

  • Misconfigured security settings on a device, potentially reducing protection without the user realizing it.

By monitoring for these behaviors—whether intentional, unintentional, or malicious—organizations can proactively detect threats, respond to incidents more effectively, and strengthen their overall security posture.

Monitoring and Reporting

A security team cannot effectively respond to threats without constant monitoring and automated reporting of key security events. This is why an auditing and alerting process is essential. Systems should be in place to automatically generate alerts when specific activities are detected and to compile daily reports with relevant security metrics.

These reports might include:

  • Phishing click rates, to track user susceptibility over time.

  • Password manager adoption, to measure secure credential storage practices.

  • Multi-factor authentication (MFA) usage, to ensure critical systems are protected with layered access controls.

  • Other indicators of risky or anomalous behavior, such as failed login attempts or unusual file access patterns.

When a user clicks a phishing link or engages in another risky action for the first time, it should trigger targeted user training. The goal is to help the user understand the mistake and avoid repeating it.

Through continuous monitoring, security teams can identify recurring risky behavior, which may suggest the need for:

  • Extended or personalized training for that user.

  • Adjustments to security settings, such as stricter access controls or data usage restrictions.

This process ensures that users are not only educated but also that security measures evolve based on real-time behavior, leading to a more resilient and informed organizational security culture.

Development and Execution of Security Plans

The process of monitoring, reporting, and training users is typically handled by the security awareness team, a specialized unit within the IT or cybersecurity department focused on user behavior and education. This team plays a critical role in strengthening the human element of an organization's security posture.

The security awareness team is responsible for:

  • Notifying the user community about emerging or ongoing security threats. This may involve sending emails, placing posters in common areas, or delivering mandatory training sessions.

  • Creating tailored training programs based on job roles, ensuring that employees receive relevant guidance. For instance, finance teams may be trained more rigorously on phishing related to invoice fraud, while IT staff may receive instruction on privilege misuse or system hardening.

  • Developing compliance-specific content when an organization is subject to regulations such as HIPAA, PCI-DSS, or GDPR. This ensures that users understand not just general threats, but also their legal responsibilities.

By using automated reporting systems, the security awareness team can generate detailed security metrics, such as:

  • Training completion rates

  • Reduction in phishing link clicks

  • Increases in secure behavior like MFA usage

These metrics allow the team to measure progress over time and identify areas where further attention is needed. If specific departments or roles continue to show vulnerabilities, the team can refocus efforts to address those risks more directly. This data-driven approach ensures that security training is not only effective but continuously improving to meet evolving threats.

The security awareness team is responsible for developing and delivering IT security training materials, which may be presented through online platforms or in-person sessions. These materials are designed to educate users about potential threats, secure behavior, and the importance of adhering to organizational policies.

In addition to training, the team also generates detailed security metrics that are shared with other IT departments and key stakeholders, such as managers and executives. These metrics provide insight into how well current security controls are performing and whether user behavior is improving as a result of the awareness efforts.

These metrics might include:

  • Phishing test results

  • Training completion rates

  • Incident reports tied to human error

  • Adoption rates of security tools like password managers or MFA

Stakeholders use this data to understand how security awareness impacts the organization’s overall risk posture. You'll often see the outcomes of these efforts in physical forms around the workplace, such as posters, flyers, or scheduled classroom training—all aimed at reinforcing good security practices.

Because the training programs are data-driven, the organization can directly correlate improvements in user behavior and reduced security incidents to the training initiatives. This feedback loop allows the security awareness team to continuously refine and tailor their efforts, ultimately contributing to a more secure organizational environment.
