Security Procedures

Change Mgt Procedures

Change management is one of the most fundamental and critical security and operational procedures in any organization. It ensures that changes to systems, configurations, or infrastructure are made in a controlled, documented, and secure manner. This helps reduce risks such as system downtime, data loss, misconfigurations, and unauthorized changes.

Purpose of Change Management

The goal of change management is to establish a structured process that:

  • Minimizes operational disruption

  • Identifies and mitigates risks in advance

  • Ensures accountability and traceability

  • Provides a path to recover from failed changes

Key Phases in the Change Management Process

  1. Scope Identification

    • Clearly define what the change entails.

    • Examples:

      • A single server update

      • Configuration change on a firewall

      • Organization-wide software upgrade

  2. Risk Assessment

    • Determine the potential impact of the change.

    • Questions to ask:

      • Is this a high-risk or low-risk change?

      • Will it affect core systems or non-critical components?

      • What dependencies could be impacted?

  3. Planning and Documentation

    • Create a formal change plan:

      • Systems or components to be modified

      • Timeline and personnel involved

      • Step-by-step execution process

    • Ensure rollback procedures are defined in case the change fails.

  4. Approval and Authorization

    • Present the change proposal to a Change Control Board (CCB) or equivalent authority.

    • The board reviews:

      • Justification for the change

      • Risk and impact analysis

      • Rollback plans and contingency measures

    • If approved, a change window is scheduled.

  5. Implementation

    • Carry out the change during the approved timeframe.

    • Follow the documented steps.

    • Ensure proper communication with stakeholders during the process.

  6. Post-Change Review and Documentation

    • Validate that the change was successful.

    • If any issues occurred, analyze the root cause.

    • Update system documentation, logs, and configuration records to reflect the changes made.

Benefits of a Proper Change Management System

  • Prevents unauthorized or ad-hoc changes

  • Reduces unplanned outages and system instability

  • Ensures compliance with regulatory or audit requirements

  • Encourages collaboration and clear communication among teams

  • Maintains a detailed audit trail for future reference

Organizations that invest in strong change management processes are better equipped to adapt securely to new needs, resolve issues efficiently, and maintain operational continuity even during complex modifications.

On-Boarding

Onboarding is the formal process of integrating a new employee or department transfer into an organization’s operational environment. For security teams, onboarding is a critical stage where access controls, policy awareness, and system configurations are correctly established to maintain organizational security and compliance.

Key Components of Security Onboarding

  1. Policy Acknowledgement

    • New hires are provided with key documents:

      • Employee Handbook

      • Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)

      • Code of Conduct

      • Data Handling and Privacy Policies

    • These policies must be read, signed, and acknowledged as part of the onboarding process to ensure legal and procedural compliance.

  2. Account Creation

    • The IT or security team creates necessary user accounts:

      • Email

      • Domain (Active Directory or equivalent)

      • Internal applications or platforms

      • VPN and remote access (if applicable)

    • This step ensures the user can authenticate and access required services securely.

  3. Access Rights and Permissions

    • Apply the principle of least privilege: grant only the access necessary for the user’s role.

    • Assign users to appropriate groups or roles for permission management.

    • Verify permissions through tools or manual reviews to prevent overprovisioning.

  4. Provisioning Equipment

    • Provide required devices:

      • Laptops or desktops

      • Mobile phones or tablets (if applicable)

      • Security tokens, smart cards, or biometric devices

    • Devices should come pre-configured with:

      • Endpoint protection software

      • Full-disk encryption

      • Configuration baselines

  5. Security Awareness Training

    • Introduce the new user to the organization’s security posture.

    • Include training on:

      • Recognizing phishing attempts

      • Safe internet and email usage

      • Incident reporting procedures

      • Use of multi-factor authentication (MFA)

  6. Audit and Logging

    • Ensure user activity is logged from day one for auditing and monitoring.

    • Set up user-specific alerts for unusual or unauthorized behavior, depending on role sensitivity.

    Benefits of a Secure Onboarding Process

  7. Minimizes security gaps from misconfigured permissions

  8. Ensures the user is aware of organizational policies

  9. Helps IT and HR teams stay aligned and compliant

  10. Supports rapid integration without compromising security

A smooth and well-secured onboarding process helps new users become productive quickly, while simultaneously protecting the integrity and confidentiality of the organization's systems and data.

Off-Boarding

Just as onboarding ensures a smooth and secure transition into an organization, offboarding provides a controlled, secure, and documented exit process for when an employee leaves the organization. A well-defined offboarding policy minimizes risks such as data breaches, unauthorized access, and loss of organizational assets.

Key Components of the Offboarding Process

  1. Initiation of Offboarding

    • Triggered by resignation, termination, contract expiration, or internal transfer.

    • Notification typically comes from HR or management, and must be formally documented.

    • A predefined checklist or offboarding workflow should be initiated immediately.

  2. Hardware Asset Recovery

    • Collect all assigned devices:

      • Laptops, desktops

      • Mobile phones

      • Access cards, security tokens, USB drives

    • Log condition, status, and return date of each asset.

    • Devices should be audited and wiped (if reissued or disposed).

  3. Data Management

    • Identify and recover business-critical data stored on user devices or cloud accounts.

    • Backup files and transfer ownership (emails, documents, projects) to designated personnel.

    • Ensure secure removal or archiving of personal data in compliance with privacy laws.

  4. Account Handling

    • Disable all accounts immediately upon exit to prevent unauthorized access.

      • Email, VPN, cloud apps, internal tools

      • Domain/Active Directory accounts

    • Do not delete accounts immediately:

      • Some files may be encrypted with that user’s credentials or certificates.

      • Retaining the account temporarily allows recovery of essential decryption keys or license bindings.

  5. Access Revocation

    • Remove:

      • Building access (badges, key fobs)

      • Remote access (VPN, SSH keys, privileged credentials)

      • Third-party services or SaaS platforms

    • Revoke all group memberships and application-specific permissions.

  6. Exit Interview and Acknowledgments

    • Ensure return of intellectual property.

    • Remind the departing user of any non-disclosure agreements (NDAs) or post-employment restrictions.

    • Capture any valuable feedback during the transition.

  7. Audit and Documentation

    • Keep logs of:

      • All removed accounts

      • Recovered devices

      • Transferred or archived data

      • Final checklist verification

    • Security or IT teams should sign off on the offboarding process.

    Best Practices

  8. Time the deactivation: Disable accounts during the final moments of employment, not before.

  9. Use automated tools to streamline account deprovisioning across platforms.

  10. Coordinate between HR, IT, and Security to avoid oversight.

  11. Establish grace periods where data is retained but access is blocked.

Why It Matters

  • Prevents insider threats or sabotage after separation

  • Protects sensitive company data from being lost or leaked

  • Ensures compliance with organizational and legal policies

  • Preserves data availability and reduces operational disruption

By implementing a structured and secure offboarding process, organizations can protect themselves from a wide range of security risks and ensure continuity in operations even as personnel change.

Playbooks

Security Playbooks and SOAR Platforms

Organizations often rely on security playbooks—structured guides that define the exact sequence of actions to take during specific security incidents. These playbooks are invaluable during high-pressure scenarios because they offer a repeatable, step-by-step process for handling events such as:

  • Data breaches – A playbook might include steps like notifying stakeholders, isolating affected systems, preserving logs, and starting forensic analysis.

  • Ransomware attacks – The process might start with disconnecting infected systems, informing legal/compliance teams, assessing backup availability, and so on.

Since every type of incident has its own nuances, organizations typically maintain multiple playbooks covering different threat scenarios—from malware infections and DDoS attacks to insider threats or unauthorized access.

To streamline responses and improve efficiency, these playbooks are increasingly being integrated into SOAR platforms—short for Security Orchestration, Automation, and Response. These platforms:

  • Integrate with multiple security tools (SIEM, firewalls, endpoint solutions, ticketing systems, etc.)

  • Allow automated execution of predefined tasks (e.g., blocking IPs, creating alerts, initiating scans)

  • Help reduce human error and accelerate response time by removing repetitive manual steps

By combining structured playbooks with automation, organizations can standardize their incident responses, reduce time-to-resolution, and free up analysts to focus on more critical and analytical tasks.

Monitoring and Revision

Continuous Improvement of Security Processes

As technology evolves and new threats emerge, it’s crucial for organizations to regularly review and revise their security processes, procedures, and tools. The goal is to stay ahead of potential vulnerabilities and ensure that the security posture remains robust.

Key Aspects of Continuous Improvement:

  1. Tightening Security Controls:

    • Organizations should frequently assess their security posture and update their controls to reflect the growing sophistication of threats. This might involve tightening the change control process to ensure only authorized changes are made, or updating policies around authentication, access controls, and data protection.

  2. Enhancing Playbooks:

    • Existing playbooks should be evaluated to identify areas where efficiency or security can be improved. This could involve refining the steps for incident response, adding new tools to the workflow, or including new best practices as security standards evolve.

    • New playbooks may need to be created for responding to threats involving new technologies, such as the introduction of IoT devices, cloud computing, or AI-driven attacks.

  3. Adapting to Emerging Threats:

    • Security teams must stay informed about the latest emerging threats. Cyber attackers are constantly adapting and finding innovative ways to exploit vulnerabilities. A good practice is to subscribe to threat intelligence feeds and monitor cybersecurity trends so playbooks can be updated to address these threats.

  4. Technology Integration:

    • As organizations grow and new technologies are integrated into the environment, the security framework must adapt to accommodate them. This might involve adopting new tools, revising security protocols, or integrating more automation to handle complex environments.

    • For example, if new cloud-based services are adopted, ensuring proper encryption and access management policies becomes crucial.

  5. Collaboration and Feedback:

    • Regular reviews and feedback loops from all stakeholders (security analysts, IT, compliance teams, etc.) are essential to continuously improve security operations. This helps identify gaps, improve response times, and create a more secure environment.

By maintaining a flexible, proactive approach to monitoring, revising, and improving security procedures, organizations can stay resilient against new threats and vulnerabilities while adapting quickly to change.

Governance Structures

In both private organizations and governmental agencies, effective governance is essential for ensuring proper decision-making, resource allocation, and alignment with broader goals. While the overarching principles remain similar, the structure and scope of governance can differ greatly based on the type of organization and its operational context.

Key Elements of Governance Structures:

  1. Board of Directors or Panel:

    • Governance begins with a board (either a board of directors or a panel of specialists). This group is typically responsible for setting broad objectives and high-level directives for the organization. In private sector organizations, these directives often focus on business goals, profitability, and stakeholder value. In governmental agencies, directives might also include legal compliance, public welfare, and regulatory requirements.

  2. Committees and Subject Matter Experts:

    • Committees are often formed to break down the tasks set by the board and to develop detailed plans for implementing these goals. These committees usually consist of subject matter experts (SMEs) in relevant areas, who analyze the issues in-depth, propose solutions, and execute the required actions.

    • In government organizations, committees may also involve representatives from various branches of government or specialized experts to deal with the complexities of public service, legal compliance, and policy development.

  3. Centralized vs. Decentralized Governance:

    • Centralized Governance: In this model, decision-making authority is concentrated within a central group or body, which oversees the entire organization. This structure often leads to faster decisions but can be less responsive to local or departmental needs.

    • Decentralized Governance: Here, decision-making authority is distributed across different departments, teams, or regions. This approach tends to allow for more local control and flexibility, as decisions can be made closer to where the work is happening. This model is particularly useful in large, complex organizations that span diverse sectors or geographic regions.

  4. Governmental Organizations:

    • In government agencies, governance often intersects with legal frameworks, administrative procedures, and political considerations. These organizations typically operate with a public mandate, meaning that their activities must be transparent and accountable to the public.

    • Public Meetings and Transparency: Unlike private organizations, government agencies generally have open meetings where the public can observe and even participate in the governance process. This ensures public accountability and maintains a direct connection between the government and the citizens it serves.

    • Political Influence: In governmental agencies, political considerations can significantly influence governance decisions, particularly when it comes to allocating resources or shaping policies. Decision-making can be affected by political agendas or public opinion, making governance in the public sector uniquely challenging.

  5. Meeting the Needs of the Organization:

    • The form of governance that is most effective depends on the nature of the organization. For example, a private corporation may thrive with a centralized governance model, where quick decisions are necessary to respond to market changes. Meanwhile, a government organization may benefit from decentralized governance, allowing local agencies or departments to better serve their specific communities while still aligning with overarching national or regional policies.

    • Ultimately, the governance structure must align with the goals of the organization and adapt to the challenges and opportunities it faces. The key is finding the balance between centralized control and local autonomy, legal compliance, and public transparency.

In both private and public sectors, a well-designed governance model facilitates effective decision-making, strategic planning, and responsive operations, ensuring that organizations are capable of achieving their goals while maintaining accountability and efficiency.
