Security Standards

In the technology industry, we rely on standards to define formal processes for handling various situations. These standards provide extensive documentation, which helps everyone understand the specific requirements involved and significantly reduces the amount of risk within the environment.

Some organizations may create their own list of security standards, especially if they have unique internal requirements. However, many prefer to use pre-established standards that have already been widely adopted and vetted.

Two major organizations that provide these kinds of security standards are:

  • ISO – The International Organization for Standardization, which creates globally recognized frameworks for information security and other domains.

  • NIST – The National Institute of Standards and Technology, which provides detailed guidelines and best practices, especially widely used in the United States.

Using these standards ensures consistency, improves compliance, and helps in managing and mitigating security risks effectively.

Password

A set of security standards that everyone in an organization must know and follow are those specifically associated with passwords. Passwords are a fundamental part of information security, and every organization typically defines formal password policies that describe what constitutes a strong password and how authentication should be managed.

Different organizations may have slightly different views on what makes a good password, but the core components are usually centered around password complexity. This includes rules on minimum length, use of uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, special characters, and so on.

Beyond complexity, the password policy may also define:

  • Authentication standards: For example, the organization might require all switches, routers, or firewalls to authenticate using a centralized method like LDAP or RADIUS, rather than relying on local device accounts.

  • Password reset procedures: To ensure account security, password changes or resets must follow a standardized and secure process, possibly requiring identity verification or administrative approval.

  • Password change frequency: Policies might enforce changing passwords every 60 or 90 days, although modern best practices often focus more on detecting breaches than frequent changes.

  • Secure password storage: Standards should ensure that passwords are stored securely (e.g., using salted hashes).

  • Approved password managers: Organizations may also list which password management tools are approved for storing and generating passwords securely.

These policies ensure that every user maintains good security hygiene and helps prevent unauthorized access through weak or compromised credentials.

Access Control

Once someone successfully authenticates to a system with their password, we need another set of standards to determine how they can access data on that system. These standards fall under the category of access control.

Access control policies specify what type of information a user can access, when they can access it, and under what conditions. These policies are essential to maintaining the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of data.

For example, an organization might have a policy that prohibits the use of Discretionary Access Control (DAC) and instead requires Mandatory Access Control (MAC), which enforces strict rules defined by the system, not by the individual data owner.

There should also be well-defined procedures for determining what level of access a specific user should have. This might include:

  • Managerial approval: Access to sensitive resources may require sign-off by a supervisor or department head.

  • Prerequisites: A user may need to complete security training or a certification course before being granted access to particular systems or data.

  • Role-based access: Permissions may be assigned based on the user’s job role using Role-Based Access Control (RBAC).

Just as it is important to control how access is granted, it's equally important to define how access is revoked. This could occur due to:

  • A security violation.

  • The expiration of an account or temporary credentials.

  • A user leaving the organization.

  • The completion or termination of a contract.

These standards help ensure that access to data is granted responsibly and revoked appropriately to reduce the risk of unauthorized data exposure.

Physical Security

Physical security is also an important consideration when creating standards for the organization, especially if your environment involves a constant flow of employees, contractors, and guests. Ensuring the safety of both people and property requires well-defined physical security policies.

For example, an organization may have a standard requiring all users to present an ID badge upon arrival. That same badge might also be used to gain access to different parts of the building through electronic door locks. These access controls could be further refined depending on the user’s role:

  • Employees might have full access to the areas they need to work in.

  • Contractors could have limited access to only certain locations and during specific hours.

  • Guests may need to be escorted or signed in by an internal employee.

Some physical security standards may go further by specifying the type of door lock mechanisms in use. For instance, a policy might require:

  • Card-based systems

  • Keypads with PINs

  • Biometric authentication (e.g., fingerprint or facial recognition)

In high-security areas, the standards might also include motion detection systems, surveillance cameras, and alarm systems for after-hours monitoring.

Standards should also cover offboarding procedures. When an individual leaves the organization, there needs to be a clear policy for:

  • Collecting and disabling ID badges

  • Revoking physical access credentials

  • Notifying security personnel

These physical security standards ensure that internal staff remain safe and that unauthorized individuals are prevented from gaining access to sensitive or restricted areas.

Encryption

Encryption Standards and Their Role in Organizational Security

Given the complexities involved in encryption technologies, it is essential for every organization to maintain well-documented encryption standards. These standards guide how encryption should be implemented, what technologies are acceptable, and how data must be protected depending on its state.

A solid encryption policy may include:

1. Hashing and Password Storage

Passwords should never be stored in plaintext. Instead, standards might require:

  • The use of cryptographic hash functions (e.g., SHA-256 or bcrypt)

  • Application of salts to hashes to prevent rainbow table attacks

  • Guidelines on how salts are generated, stored, and managed

  • Rules on password iteration counts or key stretching

2. Encryption Algorithms

The policy should specify approved encryption algorithms and their configurations, such as:

  • AES-256 for data encryption

  • RSA or ECC for asymmetric encryption

  • TLS 1.2 or 1.3 for secure network communication

  • Disallowed algorithms, like outdated DES, RC4, or MD5

3. Encryption Implementation Standards

Beyond algorithm selection, it's important to define:

  • Key generation and management practices

  • Certificate usage and renewal policies

  • Secure storage for private keys

  • Access control for encryption tools and APIs

4. Encryption by Data State

Data typically exists in three states, each with its own encryption requirements:

  • Data at Rest: Stored data (e.g., in databases, file systems)

    • May require full-disk encryption or database-level encryption

  • Data in Transit: Data moving across networks

    • Must use secure protocols like TLS, HTTPS, or VPNs

  • Data in Use: Data currently being processed in memory

    • May involve runtime protections or secure enclaves (e.g., Intel SGX)

Each state should have specific policies to ensure the appropriate level of protection.

5. Compliance and Confidentiality

Encryption standards also help:

  • Ensure regulatory compliance (e.g., GDPR, HIPAA, PCI-DSS)

  • Maintain the confidentiality, integrity, and availability (CIA) of sensitive information

  • Prevent unauthorized access by internal or external actors

By establishing comprehensive and enforced encryption standards, organizations can significantly reduce the risk of data breaches and ensure that sensitive data remains protected—regardless of how or where it is accessed.
