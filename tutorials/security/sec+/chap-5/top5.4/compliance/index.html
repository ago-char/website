Compliance

Compliance is the process of meeting a series of standards. These standards may be created by regulations or laws, or they might be from agreements made with third parties. There may be extensive compliance requirements for your organization, and many of these may depend on the type of business you operate or the laws specific to your region or country.

One of the most important considerations when dealing with compliance is that there are often penalties for non-compliance. These penalties could include fines, loss of employment for yourself or others, and in the worst cases, incarceration(imprisonment).

Compliance requirements may be based on national laws or may extend to international regulations, depending on the scope and nature of your operations.

Compliance Reporting

Many organizations perform their own internal compliance checks. Often, this responsibility is associated with a Central Compliance Officer (CCO)—an individual tasked with ensuring the entire organization complies with state, local, federal, and other applicable requirements. This office is also responsible for communicating the organization's compliance status internally and externally.

In addition to internal compliance, organizations may also face external compliance requirements, particularly when working with third parties that impose specific standards. These situations often involve ongoing reporting, which may include generating a compliance report annually or at intervals defined by the applicable compliance framework.

If reporting is inaccurate or a required reporting period is missed, the organization may face penalties or sanctions as a result of the oversight.

Example of Regulatory Compliance

A good example of regulatory compliance is the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX), formally known as the Public Company Accounting Reform and Investor Protection Act of 2002. This regulation is designed to protect investors by improving the accuracy and reliability of corporate disclosures.

In the healthcare field, compliance with HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) is critical. HIPAA ensures that individuals' medical information in the United States remains private and secure.

Another regulatory compliance example is the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA) of 1999. This act requires financial institutions to explain their information-sharing practices and to safeguard sensitive data. If you’re in the U.S., you may occasionally receive a notice from your financial institution outlining their privacy policy—this is a direct result of GLBA requirements.

What if you are not in Compliance ? - Well that's a bad place to be

We mentioned earlier that there can be significant penalties for being out of compliance. A good example of this is HIPAA non-compliance fines and sanctions. It’s important to understand the consequences of failing to comply with such regulations.

  • A basic violation could result in a fine of up to $50,000, up to 1 year in prison, or both. This would be classified as a Class A misdemeanor or basic felony.

  • If the non-compliance was committed under false pretenses, the penalty increases to a fine of up to $100,000, up to 5 years in prison, or both. This would be considered a Class C felony.

  • If there is intent to sell, transfer, or use individually identifiable health information for commercial advantage, personal gain, or malicious harm, the penalty is even more severe—with a fine of up to $250,000, up to 10 years in prison, or both. This is a Class A felony.

In addition to criminal penalties, there are also civil fines. The maximum is $100 per violation, with a total not to exceed $25,000 for all violations of an identical requirement.

This is a clear example of why organizations invest significant time and resources to ensure full compliance with regulatory standards.

Reputaional Damage because of Non-Compliance

There is also reputational damage that might occur if an organization falls out of compliance. Many states have mandatory disclosure requirements if an organization is hacked or breached, and the act of disclosing such an incident can significantly harm the company’s image. In some cases, it can even lead to a temporary drop in stock prices, reflecting the loss of stakeholder trust.

A strong example of how reputational damage can impact a company began in October 2016, when Uber experienced a breach in which 25.6 million names, email addresses, and phone numbers were extracted from their systems. However, Uber did not publicly announce the breach until November 2017, more than a year later. During that time, Uber allegedly paid the hackers $100,000 and had them sign a Non-Disclosure Agreement in an effort to suppress the incident.

This decision ultimately caught up with the company. In 2018, Uber was fined $148 million for failing to disclose the breach in a timely manner. The hackers pled guilty in October 2019, and in May 2023, Uber’s former Chief Security Officer was sentenced to 3 years probation and a $50,000 fine.

Had Uber followed legal disclosure requirements at the time of the breach, it could have remained in compliance. Instead, the attempt to cover it up resulted in financial penalties and significant reputational harm, demonstrating the long-term consequences of non-compliance.

Other Consequences of being Non-Compliance

These aren't the only consequences that can arise from non-compliance. A company may also lose a specific license tied to that compliance requirement. This can be a significant financial blow, particularly if that license is essential for selling the company's product or service. Without it, operations may be disrupted or halted entirely, leading to revenue loss and potential layoffs.

In addition, other organizations may be prohibited from conducting business with a company that has been sanctioned for non-compliance. This can cause a loss of clients or partnerships and reduce market opportunities. Regaining a revoked license can also be costly and time-consuming, adding further strain to the business.

Some forms of compliance are governed by contractual agreements between two private organizations. In such cases, if a company fails to maintain compliance, it may be considered a breach of contract. This can lead to termination of the business relationship or financial penalties. However, since these agreements are between private entities, the issue may sometimes be resolved without legal proceedings, through negotiation or internal remediation.

Compliance Monitoring

You can see how being out of compliance can negatively impact an organization, which is why many companies assign specific individuals to focus on compliance monitoring. In this context, two important terms often used are due diligence and due care. These describe how a company demonstrates its commitment to compliance.

Due care refers to the internal actions a company takes to ensure it is following compliance requirements—such as implementing policies, training staff, and maintaining secure systems. In contrast, due diligence applies when working with external parties and involves verifying that those third parties are also complying with relevant standards or agreements.

In many cases, a high-level executive is responsible for signing off on the organization's compliance status. This act of formal approval is known as Attestation and Acknowledgment, and it signifies that the executive has reviewed the compliance reports and is affirming the company’s compliance status in good health. The executive is ultimately accountable for ensuring compliance is properly maintained.

For larger companies offering multiple products and services, compliance requirements can be extensive. To keep up, organizations typically engage in continuous monitoring. This is often managed through internal tracking tools that monitor the status of all compliance-related tasks. Some organizations may need to reach out to external sources to verify their compliance status.

Given the scale and complexity, many companies now look to automate the compliance process. Automation can streamline the collection of data from internal teams, third parties, and various systems across the organization. These automated compliance monitoring systems help compile reports, maintain real-time status updates, and ensure the company stays current with all its compliance obligations. The specific approach and tools used can vary widely depending on the type and need of business and industry.
