Agreement Types

Service Level Agreement (SLA)

If you're working with a service provider, you may want to set certain requirements for uptime or availability, and you can integrate those requirements into a document called a Service Level Agreement (SLA). This allows you to set minimum terms for the service provided by the third party.

Example:
When entering into a contract with an Internet Service Provider (ISP), you might specify:

  • No more than 4 hours of unscheduled downtime

  • A technician must be dispatched during outages

  • Spare equipment is to be kept on-site by the ISP

All of these requirements can be written into the SLA so both parties clearly understand the expectations.

Memorandum of Understanding (MOU)

When starting a partnership with a third party, you might begin with a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU).

  • MOUs outline broad goals of what the two organizations hope to accomplish

  • May include confidentiality statements

  • Typically an informal document

  • Not a signed contract and not legally enforceable

It serves as a general overview of a potential partnership.

Memorandum of Agreement (MOA)

An MOA is a step above the MOU. It describes the relationship between two organizations in more detail.

  • May include legally binding clauses, though it is generally not a formal contract

  • Clarifies how the two parties will work together

MOAs are more specific than MOUs, but still often lack full legal enforceability.

Master Service Agreement (MSA)

If you plan to have an ongoing relationship with a third party, a Master Service Agreement (MSA) is often used.

  • A formal legal contract

  • Sets terms and expectations for both parties

  • Used as a framework for future tasks or services

Example:
The MSA might outline:

  • Service terms

  • Billing methods

  • Payment schedules

Additional services are added later without renegotiating the core agreement.

Statement of Work (SOW)

Used in conjunction with an MSA, the Statement of Work (SOW) defines specific tasks.

Includes details such as:

  • Scope of the job

  • Job location

  • Deliverable schedule

  • Expected outcomes

SOWs ensure clarity and provide a point of reference if questions arise later about what was or wasnâ€™t completed.

Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)

To protect confidential information between two organizations, a Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA) is used.

  • Allows parties to share trade secrets or business plans securely

  • Specifies what must remain confidential

  • Is a formal contract signed before sensitive discussions begin

Types of NDAs:

  • Unilateral NDA: Only one party must maintain confidentiality

  • Bilateral/Mutual NDA: Both parties are required to maintain confidentiality

  • Multilateral NDA: More than two parties are involved

Business Partnership Agreement (BPA)

For formal partnerships, a Business Partnership Agreement (BPA) outlines the financial and operational structure.

Includes:

  • Ownership stakes

  • Decision-making authority

  • Business operation details

Also outlines contingency plans, such as:

  • What happens if financial disputes occur

  • What to do if a disaster shuts down operations

A BPA ensures both sides are prepared for both routine operations and unexpected events.
