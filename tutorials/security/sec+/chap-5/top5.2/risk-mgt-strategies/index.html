Risk Management Strategies

Organizations may adopt different strategies to handle risks effectively. The primary strategies include:

1. Risk Transfer

  • Definition: Shifting the risk to another party.

  • Example: Purchasing cybersecurity insurance transfers the financial burden of certain incidents to the insurer.

2. Risk Acceptance

  • Definition: The organization chooses to accept the risk without taking additional actions to mitigate it.

  • Usage: Common when the cost of mitigation is higher than the potential impact.

  • Example 1:

    • A manufacturing device runs an outdated Windows OS.

    • The manufacturer does not support patching.

    • Company policy requires monthly updates, but an exemption is granted.

    • Condition: Device must remain offline and isolated from the network. And approval needed from high level officers or BOD in some cases.

  • Example 2:

    • Policy: All systems must be patched within 3 days.

    • Issue: A patch causes a critical software crash.

    • Solution: An exception is granted to delay patching until the software is updated.

3. Risk Avoidance

  • Definition: Completely eliminating the risk by avoiding the activity or condition that introduces it.

  • Effect: Removes the need for mitigation since the risk no longer exists.

  • Example: Deciding not to use a high-risk application or service.

4. Risk Mitigation

  • Definition: Reducing the impact or likelihood of the risk.

  • Example:

    • Concern: Threats from the internet.

    • Mitigation: Implement a next-generation firewall to filter and monitor network traffic.

This structured approach makes it easier for decision-makers to evaluate and apply the most appropriate strategy based on business priorities and constraints.

Risk Reporting

An organization may have tens or even hundreds of risks that need to be tracked, and one effective way to manage them is through risk reporting.

  • Purpose:
    Risk reporting creates a list of all risks the company is tracking.
    It provides a description of each risk and outlines how to handle them.

  • Usage:
    This document is commonly referenced by upper management, especially when making key business decisions such as purchases or evaluating strategic risks.

  • Nature:

    • It is a living document, meaning it is constantly updated.

    • It includes:

      • Critical risks

      • Emerging risks

  • Significance:
    It ensures that decision-makers are informed and aware of potential impacts while steering the organization forward.
