Threat Intelligence

  • You need to do your own research on the latest threats.

  • Not only on threats but also on threat actors, their tools, and techniques.

  • Consider visiting hacker groups, consulting researchers (internal or external), and networking with professionals in the same field.

  • Fortunately, there are various websites offering this information—some free, others paid.

OSINT

  • One of the best places to start is Open Source Intelligence (OSINT).

  • This is publicly available data that you can acquire without legal barriers.

  • Sources include:

    • Internet forums

    • Discussion groups

    • Social media

    • Government websites, hearings, reports

    • Publicly shared commercial data

  • Some websites aggregate and centralize these resources to streamline the search process.

Third-Party Intelligence

  • These are proprietary organizations that specialize in threat intelligence.

  • They continuously monitor and report the latest threats.

  • Access to their data usually requires a paid subscription.

  • Their reports are:

    • Easy to understand

    • Well formatted

    • Correlated for better insight

  • You can choose threat categories relevant to your environment.

  • They often provide solutions and action plans.

  • Their methods include manual and automated processes to detect and mitigate threats.

  • You can even outsource your entire threat intelligence efforts to them.

Information Sharing Organizations

  • These organizations sell or share security-related information.

  • Sources include:

    • Public intelligence

    • Classified data

    • Private reports from various organizations (as per agreement)

  • They offer:

    • Real-time monitoring insights

    • Identification of attacker tactics and severity of threats

    • Solutions used by others in response

  • Cyber Threat Alliance (CTA) is one such group. Its members:

    • Upload threat intelligence in a specific format

    • Validate and confirm each submission

    • Make it available to others in the alliance

Dark Web

  • This is often the home of hackers.

  • It includes listings and discussions on:

    • Leaked information

    • Hacking tools

    • Passwords, accounts, credit card data

  • Monitoring their forums and marketplaces can help you:

    • Identify trends

    • Understand patterns of targeting

    • Detect if your organization is being discussed

  • Use Tor Browser to access it—make sure it’s isolated and secure.

  • Always verify the authenticity of sources, as fake sites are common in that space.
