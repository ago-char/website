Analyzing Vulnerabilities

False Positives

  • Occur when the scanner flags something that is not actually a vulnerability.

  • Can waste time and resources if not identified and managed properly.

Low Severity

  • Vulnerabilities that pose minimal risk.

  • Typically may not require immediate attention but should still be monitored.

  • Often not likely to be exploited in the short term.

False Negatives

  • Occur when the scanner fails to detect a real vulnerability.

  • Can be risky because they give a false sense of security.

  • Requires ongoing vigilance and a multi-layered security approach.

Update Your Signatures

  • Regularly updating vulnerability signatures is crucial to stay current with new threats.

  • Ensure the scanner's fingerprinting mechanism is up-to-date to catch the latest vulnerabilities.

Prioritizing Vulnerabilities

  • Not all vulnerabilities are the same; some are more critical than others.

  • Use publicly available databases to help prioritize vulnerabilities.

NIST

  • Synced with CVE (Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures).

  • Offers enhanced search functionality to help prioritize vulnerabilities.

CVE

  • Resources like nist, cve.mitre.org, and Microsoft security bulletins are reliable sources for prioritizing vulnerabilities.

  • Vendor-specific advisories should also be checked regularly.

Vulnerability Scanner DB

  • Vulnerability scanners rely on signatures and fingerprints of apps to detect vulnerabilities.

  • Ensure that the scanner's database is updated regularly to stay ahead of new vulnerabilities.

Patch Planning

Exposure Factor

  • Represents the loss of value or business activity if the vulnerability is exploited.

  • Expressed as a percentage.

  • Example: If a DDoS attack could cause a server to be down 50% of the time, the exposure factor would be 50%.

  • For a server with a vulnerability that could completely disable it, the exposure factor would be 100%.

Environment Variables

  • Not all environments are the same; some are on the cloud, some are on-premises, and others may be external, internal, or honeypots.

  • Prioritize patching based on the environment:

    • Public servers should be patched immediately.

    • Internal networks may have more time, especially if they are isolated from external threats.

Industry/Organizational Impact

  • A vulnerability with a low priority may still require immediate attention if it impacts your organization's ability to operate.

  • Different organizations will experience different impacts from the same vulnerability, depending on their infrastructure, operations, and tolerance for downtime.

Risk Tolerance

  • The amount of risk the organization is willing to accept for a particular vulnerability.

  • Example: If exposure is low (e.g., 10%), the organization may decide to take the risk and delay patching.

  • Risk tolerance is influenced by factors like:

    • Downtime and its financial impact

    • The cost of patching and testing

    • Potential gain vs. potential loss

  • Testing Policy:

    • The extent of testing needed before deploying patches varies.

    • Insufficient testing could lead to system crashes, while excessive testing could delay the patch and increase vulnerability.

    • A balanced approach is required to minimize risks while keeping the organization operational.
