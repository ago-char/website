Asset Management

  • A formal process is followed for purchasing goods from third parties.

  • It requires approval and signatures from different departments.

  • An end user typically initiates the request, which goes to the purchasing department and ultimately to the management team.

  • Negotiations with suppliers involve pricing, licensing, terms, and contracts.

  • Payments may be made upon delivery or after use, depending on the agreed terms and invoice.

Accounting

  • Tangible goods are added to the Central Asset Tracking System.

  • For example, when someone receives a laptop, the ownership is registered in their name; you can also update or modify details if needed.

  • Assets are classified based on type—hardware or software.

  • This classification helps in financial accounting. For instance, hardware may be treated as a capital expenditure (CapEx), while software may be categorized as an operating expenditure (OpEx).

Asset Tracking

  • Maintaining an inventory of assets helps in tracking their physical location.

  • It helps you identify where the device is—whether it's a building, room number, tag, or assigned employee.

  • Asset tracking can be part of enumeration. For example, a switch may include connected cables, extended ports, etc.

  • Devices may be tagged using RFID, barcodes, organization names, or other identification methods. Some important devices even use GPS or location tracking systems.

Reuse of Devices / Media Sanitization

  • Reusing devices can be challenging, especially if they have storage media attached.

  • This is mainly due to data sensitivity.

  • The process of data destruction may differ depending on the use case:

    • If you are giving it to a recycling team (including the hard drive), you should fully wipe or destroy the data.

    • Always consider backing up first.

    • The same precautions apply if you're selling or handing over the device to someone else.

    • In internal transfers, sensitive files may need deletion or the drive may be replaced altogether.

  • Use secure data wiping tools and ensure no data can be recovered afterward.

Certificate of Destruction

  • Organizations often have thousands of devices and drives to destroy.

  • They might lack time, resources, or an internal department for hardware destruction.

  • Therefore, they partner with third-party vendors that specialize in this task.

  • These vendors not only destroy the drives but also provide a Certificate of Destruction.

  • Once certified, you can be confident the data is no longer accessible.

Data Retention

  • Before initiating destruction, always ensure proper data backup.

  • Different types of data require different backup methods—full, partial, or incremental.

  • Backup formats may vary: physical ledgers, computer files, drives, etc.

  • Retention policies must be followed carefully.

    • Example: Your organization may have a policy to retain emails for two years. Even if they appear outdated, they cannot be deleted.

    • Emails may be retrieved from drives in real-time if hosted locally.

    • Financial or manual records may need to be kept in paper form (contracts, sales records, etc.) for extended periods.
